Lab Safety & iNaturalist

Biology II Lab begins with foundational lab safety protocols and an introduction to iNaturalist, a citizen science platform used throughout the semester to document and identify organisms in their natural habitats. Observations made in iNaturalist become part of a global biodiversity database and help students develop field identification skills.

iNaturalist Essentials

Each observation needs a photo, date, and location. The app uses AI-assisted identification suggestions, but community verification brings observations to "Research Grade" status (requires photo + location + date + 2/3 community agreement on ID). Your observations contribute to real biodiversity research through the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF).

Safety PrincipleKey Details
Specimen handlingAlways wear gloves when handling preserved specimens; many are fixed in formalin or stored in ethanol
Dissection toolsCut away from your body; scalpels and razor blades go in sharps containers, never in regular trash
Microscope careCarry with both hands (arm and base); start at lowest magnification; clean lenses with lens paper only
Chemical disposalNever pour chemicals down the drain unless instructed; use designated waste containers
Broken glassUse a broom and dustpan, never bare hands; dispose in designated glass waste containers
Florida & Labster Biodiversity

Early labs use Labster virtual simulations alongside field observations to explore Florida's remarkable biodiversity. Northwest Florida features coastal dune ecosystems, longleaf pine savannas, seagrass beds, and freshwater bayous, each supporting distinct communities of prokaryotes, protists, fungi, plants, and animals studied throughout the semester.

Prokaryotes

Prokaryotes are the most ancient, abundant, and metabolically diverse organisms on Earth. They lack membrane-bound nuclei and organelles but possess remarkable adaptations for survival in virtually every environment. The two domains of prokaryotic life, Bacteria and Archaea, are as genetically different from each other as either is from Eukarya.

Gram Staining

Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer that retains the crystal violet stain (appearing purple). Gram-negative bacteria have a thin peptidoglycan layer surrounded by an outer membrane, so the crystal violet washes away and they take up the safranin counterstain (appearing pink/red). The outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria contains lipopolysaccharide (LPS), which can trigger strong immune responses.

Metabolic Diversity Mnemonic

"Photo" = light energy, "Chemo" = chemical energy, "Auto" = CO₂ as carbon source, "Hetero" = organic carbon source. Combine them: a photoautotroph uses light energy to fix CO₂ (cyanobacteria), a chemoautotroph uses inorganic chemical reactions to fix CO₂ (nitrifying bacteria), and a chemoheterotroph uses organic molecules for both energy and carbon (most bacteria you encounter).

Prokaryotic reproduction is primarily through binary fission, producing genetically identical daughter cells. Genetic diversity arises through three mechanisms of horizontal gene transfer: transformation (uptake of free DNA from the environment), transduction (transfer via bacteriophages), and conjugation (direct transfer through a pilus, often carrying plasmid DNA including antibiotic resistance genes).

Protists

Protists are a paraphyletic grab-bag of eukaryotic organisms that are not plants, animals, or fungi. They range from unicellular amoebas to giant multicellular kelps. Understanding protists means understanding the diversity of eukaryotic lineages and the endosymbiotic events that shaped them.

Endosymbiotic Theory

Mitochondria originated from an alpha-proteobacterium engulfed by an ancestral eukaryote (primary endosymbiosis). Chloroplasts originated from a cyanobacterium engulfed by a eukaryote. Both organelles retain their own circular DNA, have double membranes, replicate independently, and have ribosomes similar to bacteria. Secondary endosymbiosis (a eukaryote engulfing a photosynthetic eukaryote) produced groups like brown algae and euglenids, sometimes resulting in three or four membranes around the chloroplast.

Protist Movement Types

Pseudopods = Amoeba (cytoplasmic extensions), Cilia = Paramecium (short, numerous, coordinated beating), Flagella = Euglena and Trypanosoma (long whip-like), No movement = Plasmodium (obligate intracellular parasite, relies on host).

In lab you will observe living and prepared protist specimens under the microscope, identifying key structures such as the contractile vacuole (osmoregulation) and food vacuoles (digestion) in Paramecium, the eyespot (phototaxis) and pellicle (flexible protein strips) in Euglena, and the test (shell) structures in foraminifera.

Seedless Plants

The move from water to land required critical adaptations. Seedless plants include the bryophytes (non-vascular) and the seedless vascular plants, representing two major steps in this evolutionary transition. All land plants share a defining feature: alternation of generations between a multicellular diploid sporophyte and a multicellular haploid gametophyte.

GroupVascular Tissue?Dominant GenerationExamples
Bryophytes (mosses)NoGametophyte (the green "plant" you see)Mosses, liverworts, hornworts
LycophytesYesSporophyteClub mosses (Lycopodium), spike mosses, quillworts
MonilophytesYes (ferns, horsetails, whisk ferns)SporophyteFerns, horsetails (Equisetum)
Key Adaptations for Land

Cuticle (waxy coating preventing water loss), stomata (gas exchange pores), sporopollenin (tough spore walls), gametangia (protective structures around gametes: archegonia for eggs, antheridia for sperm), and in vascular plants, xylem (water transport, dead cells) and phloem (sugar transport, living cells).

Fern Life Cycle

The mature fern you see is the sporophyte (2n). On the underside of fronds, clusters of sporangia called sori produce spores by meiosis. Spores germinate into a tiny heart-shaped prothallus (the gametophyte, 1n), which produces eggs and swimming sperm. Fertilization requires water. The new sporophyte grows from the gametophyte and eventually becomes the dominant plant.

Seedless plants still require water for reproduction because their sperm are flagellated and must swim to the egg. This is why bryophytes and ferns are most abundant in moist habitats. In lab, look for sori on fern fronds, identify the heart-shaped fern gametophyte, and compare the tiny sporophyte of a moss (the stalk with a capsule growing from the leafy gametophyte).

Seed Plants

Seeds and pollen were game-changing innovations that freed plants from dependence on water for reproduction. Seed plants include the gymnosperms (naked seeds) and angiosperms (seeds enclosed in fruits). Together they dominate terrestrial ecosystems.

Double Fertilization (Angiosperms Only)

One sperm fuses with the egg to form the zygote (2n). The other sperm fuses with the two polar nuclei to form the endosperm (3n), which nourishes the developing embryo. This is unique to angiosperms and ensures the plant invests nutrition only in fertilized ovules.

In lab, you will examine pine cone structure (ovulate vs. pollen cones), dissect flowers to identify sepals, petals, stamens (anther + filament), and carpels (stigma, style, ovary), and compare monocot and eudicot anatomy in cross-sections of stems and leaves.

Fungi & Metazoa Introduction

Fungi and animals are both opisthokonts, meaning they share a common ancestor characterized by a posterior flagellum on motile cells. Fungi are heterotrophic absorbers that decompose organic material externally and then absorb the nutrients. They are essential decomposers, plant symbionts, and occasionally pathogens.

Symbiotic Relationships

Mycorrhizae = fungus + plant roots (fungus provides minerals, especially phosphorus; plant provides sugars). Lichens = fungus + photosynthetic partner (green alga or cyanobacterium); the fungus provides structure and moisture retention, the photobiont provides carbohydrates. Lichens are sensitive to air pollution and serve as bioindicators.

The Metazoa (animals) are multicellular, heterotrophic ingestors that lack cell walls and (in most lineages) have specialized tissues organized by embryonic germ layers. All animals share a common ancestor that was likely a colonial choanoflagellate-like protist. Key animal synapomorphies include collagen-based extracellular matrix, true multicellularity with cell junctions (tight junctions, gap junctions, desmosomes), and nervous/muscle tissue in most lineages.

Lophotrochozoa

Lophotrochozoa is a major clade of protostome animals named for two features found in various members: the lophophore (a tentacle-bearing feeding structure) and the trochophore larva (a free-swimming larval stage). This clade includes an enormous diversity of body plans, from flatworms to octopuses.

PhylumKey FeaturesBody CavityExamples
Platyhelminthes (flatworms)Dorso-ventrally flattened; gastrovascular cavity with one opening; no circulatory or respiratory system; cephalizationAcoelomatePlanaria, tapeworms, flukes
RotiferaCrown of cilia (corona) for feeding; pseudocoelom; microscopic; complete digestive tractPseudocoelomateBdelloid rotifers
MolluscaMuscular foot, visceral mass, mantle (secretes shell); open or closed circulatory system; radula in mostCoelomateSnails, clams, squid, octopus

Mollusc Classes to Know

Gastropoda (snails, slugs): torsion of visceral mass, single shell or shell-less, radula for scraping. Bivalvia (clams, mussels, oysters): two hinged shells, filter feeders using gills, no head or radula. Cephalopoda (squid, octopus, nautilus): closed circulatory system, complex eyes, jet propulsion, highly intelligent; reduced or absent shell in most modern species.

Body Cavity Progression

Acoelomate = no body cavity, solid mesoderm (flatworms). Pseudocoelomate = body cavity between mesoderm and endoderm, not fully lined by mesoderm (roundworms, rotifers). Coelomate = true body cavity completely lined by mesoderm-derived peritoneum (annelids, molluscs, arthropods, chordates).

In lab, you will dissect or examine earthworms (noting segmentation, clitellum, dorsal blood vessel, crop, gizzard, nephridia) and observe mollusc diversity through shells and preserved specimens. Dissections of bivalves reveal the mantle, gills, adductor muscles, and foot.

Ecdysozoa

Ecdysozoans are protostome animals that grow by molting (ecdysis), shedding an external cuticle to allow the body to expand. This clade includes the two most species-rich animal phyla on Earth: Nematoda and Arthropoda.

Insect Metamorphosis

Complete metamorphosis (holometabolous): egg → larva → pupa → adult. The larval and adult stages are morphologically distinct and often exploit different resources (butterflies, beetles, flies, bees). Incomplete metamorphosis (hemimetabolous): egg → nymph → adult. Nymphs resemble small adults and gradually acquire adult features through successive molts (grasshoppers, dragonflies, true bugs).

Counting Arthropod Legs

"6-8-10-lots": Insects have 6 legs, arachnids have 8, crustacean decapods have 10, and myriapods have lots. Remember, crustaceans are the only arthropods with 2 pairs of antennae.

Arthropods are the most species-rich animal phylum, with insects alone accounting for over 80% of all known animal species. Their success is attributed to the versatility of jointed, chitinous exoskeletons, flight (insects are the only invertebrates that fly), diverse mouthparts, and complete metamorphosis allowing niche partitioning between life stages.

Deuterostomes: Echinoderms

Deuterostomes develop differently from protostomes: the blastopore becomes the anus (mouth forms second), cleavage is radial and indeterminate, and the coelom forms by enterocoely (outpocketing of the gut). Echinoderms are the major invertebrate deuterostome phylum and are more closely related to chordates than to any protostome group.

Protostome vs. Deuterostome Development

Protostomes: blastopore → mouth, spiral determinate cleavage, schizocoely (coelom from splitting mesoderm). Deuterostomes: blastopore → anus, radial indeterminate cleavage, enterocoely (coelom from gut outpockets). This fundamental split divides most bilateral animals into two great lineages.

Deuterostomes: Chordates

Chordates are defined by four synapomorphies present at some stage of development: a notochord (flexible rod providing support), a dorsal hollow nerve cord (becomes the brain and spinal cord in vertebrates), pharyngeal slits (become gills in fish, modified in terrestrial vertebrates), and a post-anal tail.

The Amniotic Egg

The evolution of the amniotic egg freed vertebrates from water for reproduction. Four extraembryonic membranes: amnion (fluid-filled sac around embryo), chorion (gas exchange), allantois (waste storage and gas exchange), yolk sac (nutrition). Reptiles, birds, and mammals (including egg-laying monotremes) are all amniotes.

Ecology

Ecology is the study of interactions between organisms and their environment at multiple levels of organization: individual, population, community, ecosystem, and biosphere. The ecology unit in lab connects the organismal diversity studied throughout the semester to how these organisms interact in real ecosystems.

LevelFocusKey Concepts
PopulationSingle species in an areaDensity, dispersion patterns (clumped, uniform, random), growth models (exponential vs. logistic), carrying capacity (K), r vs. K selection
CommunityInteracting speciesCompetition, predation, mutualism, parasitism, commensalism; species diversity, keystone species, ecological succession
EcosystemCommunity + abiotic factorsEnergy flow (trophic levels, 10% rule), nutrient cycling (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus), food webs, primary productivity
BiosphereAll ecosystems globallyBiomes, climate patterns, global biogeochemical cycles
Energy Flow

Energy enters ecosystems through primary producers (photosynthesis or chemosynthesis). Only about 10% of energy is transferred between trophic levels; the rest is lost as heat through cellular respiration. This is why food chains rarely exceed 4-5 trophic levels and why there are fewer top predators than herbivores. Gross primary productivity (GPP) minus respiration equals net primary productivity (NPP), the energy available to consumers.

Succession Types

Primary succession begins on bare substrate with no soil (lava flows, retreating glaciers); pioneer species like lichens and mosses build soil over time. Secondary succession occurs after a disturbance that destroys the community but leaves soil intact (fires, logging, abandoned farmland); proceeds faster because soil and seed banks remain.
